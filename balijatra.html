History of Bali Jatra

Bali Jatra, literally meaning “Voyage to Bali,” is one of Cuttack’s most iconic and historic festivals, celebrated every year on the banks of the Mahanadi River during Kartik Purnima (November). It commemorates Odisha’s ancient maritime legacy when traders and sailors, called Sadhabas, sailed from ports like Cuttack to distant lands such as Bali, Java, Sumatra, and Sri Lanka for trade and cultural exchange over a thousand years ago. On this day, people perform Boita Bandana, floating miniature boats made of banana stem, paper, or cork with diyas and betel leaves, symbolizing the voyages of their ancestors. The festival has evolved into one of Asia’s largest open-air fairs, featuring thousands of stalls selling handicrafts, silver filigree work, traditional foods, and modern goods. Cultural performances, folk dances, maritime exhibitions, and rides add to its carnival-like atmosphere. Bali Jatra thus stands as a living reminder of Odisha’s seafaring past, blending history, tradition, and community celebration on a massive scale in Cuttack.

Why It Is Famous

Bali Jatra has gained immense fame as one of Asia's largest trade fairs and cultural festivals. The festival is renowned for its massive scale, featuring thousands of stalls selling everything from traditional handicrafts to modern goods, delicious street food, and cultural performances. The festival ground transforms into a vibrant marketplace that recreates the ancient trading atmosphere of medieval Kalinga. The festival is famous for its unique boat-shaped stalls called "Boita," which symbolize the ancient trading vessels. Visitors can enjoy traditional Odia cuisine, witness spectacular cultural performances, and participate in various competitions and exhibitions. The highlight of the festival is the "Boita Bandana" ritual, where people float miniature boats made of banana bark in rivers and ponds, commemorating their ancestors' maritime adventures. Bali Jatra serves as a bridge between the past and present, preserving ancient maritime traditions while embracing modern commercial activities. The festival's fame extends beyond Odisha, attracting international visitors and researchers interested in maritime history and cultural heritage. It has become a symbol of Odisha's rich cultural legacy and its historical connections with Southeast Asia.
