Joyful Celebrations in Cuttack

The Festival of Kites

Makar Sankranti, celebrated on January 14th each year, marks the transition of the sun into the zodiac sign of Capricorn (Makara). It is a significant harvest festival in India, symbolizing the end of winter and the beginning of longer days. The festival is known by various names across different regions, such as Pongal in Tamil Nadu, Lohri in Punjab, and Uttarayan in Gujarat. Makar Sankranti is characterized by vibrant kite flying, traditional rituals, and the preparation of special sweets made from sesame seeds (til) and jaggery (gur), symbolizing warmth and togetherness.

In Cuttack, Makar Sankranti is celebrated with devotion, and a vibrant community spirit. People wake up early in the morning to take holy baths in rivers or ponds, believing it purifies the body and soul, and then visit temples such as the famous Katak Chandi Temple to offer prayers and seek blessings. Homes are filled with the aroma of traditional delicacies like Makar Chaula, Arisa Pitha, Kakara Pitha, and Chakuli Pitha, which are prepared and shared with family, friends, and neighbors. Charity plays a central role in the festival, with people donating rice, sesame, jaggery, and clothes to the poor, reflecting the festival‚Äôs spirit of giving. The day is also marked by kite flying, joyful gatherings, and cultural activities in different neighborhoods. These rituals, combined with the exchange of festive foods and community bonding, give Makar Sankranti in Cuttack a warm, lively, and deeply traditional atmosphere.
