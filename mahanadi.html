History of Mahanadi

The Mahanadi, meaning "Great River" in Sanskrit, is one of the major rivers of peninsular India and holds immense historical, cultural, and economic significance. Originating from the highlands of Chhattisgarh near Sihawa in Dhamtari district, this mighty river flows eastward for 858 kilometers before emptying into the Bay of Bengal.

Ancient texts and inscriptions dating back to the 2nd century BCE mention Mahanadi as "Mahananda," highlighting its significance in early Indian civilization and trade routes.

Throughout history, the Mahanadi has been the backbone of agricultural prosperity in the region. The river has witnessed the rise and fall of numerous dynasties, including the Kalinga Empire, which flourished along its banks. The famous Kalinga War fought by Emperor Ashoka took place in the delta region of this river, marking a turning point in Indian history.
